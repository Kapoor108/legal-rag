### Q81
**Question:** What is the definition of gross income under Section 61 of the Internal Revenue Code?

### Q83
**Question:** What is the three-part test from Commissioner v. Glenshaw Glass Co. for determining gross income?

**Answer:** The three-part test from Glenshaw Glass defines gross income as: (1) undeniable accessions to wealth, (2) clearly realized, and (3) over which the taxpayers have complete dominion. All three elements must be present.

### Q84
**Question:** Are punitive damages taxable as gross income?

**Answer:** Yes. Under Commissioner v. Glenshaw Glass Co. (348 U.S. 426), punitive damages constitute gross income. Congress intended to tax all gains except those specifically exempted. Punitive damages are undeniable accessions to wealth, clearly realized, and over which the taxpayers have complete dominion. The fact they are extracted as punishment does not change their taxable character.
[Commissioner Of Internal Revenue V. Glenshaw Glass Co., 348 U.S. 426, p.2]

### Q85
**Question:** What was the taxpayers' argument in Glenshaw Glass and why did the Court reject it?

**Answer:** The taxpayers argued that punitive damages are windfalls and do not constitute income because they represent a penalty imposed on the wrongdoer rather than a gain derived from capital or labor. The Supreme Court rejected this argument, holding that Congress intended to tax all gains except those specifically exempted, and punitive damages are not a gift nor statutorily excluded.

### Q86
**Question:** What is the standard for a tax realization event under Cottage Savings?

**Answer:** Under Cottage Savings Association v. Commissioner (499 U.S. 554), a realization event occurs under Section 1001(a) when properties exchanged are "materially different." Properties are materially different if their respective possessors enjoy legal entitlements that are different in kind or extent — for example, different obligors or different collateral securing the instruments.
[Cottage Savings Association V. Commissioner Of Internal Revenue, 499 U.S. 554, p.2]

### Q87

**Answer:** Yes. The Supreme Court held that Cottage Savings realized a deductible loss. Because the mortgage participation interests exchanged had different obligors and were secured by different homes, they embodied distinct legal entitlements and were therefore "materially different," triggering a realization event under Section 1001(a).

### Q92
**Question:** What did Eisner v. Macomber establish, and how was it limited by later cases?

**Answer:** Eisner v.
